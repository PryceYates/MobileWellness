Overview

Glucose levels and daily movement

A study tracking continuous glucose readings and daily step counts side by side in healthy, nondiabetic adults over two weeks.

What the study looked at

Forty participants without diabetes wore a continuous glucose monitor and a fitness tracker for 14 days under normal, everyday conditions. Researchers recorded glucose readings and daily step counts side by side, and also collected detailed information on diet and eating behavior through structured questionnaires, then looked at how these factors related to each other across different body mass index categories.

What they found

Even in people without diabetes, blood glucose doesn't stay flat all day. It rises and falls in response to meals, and how much it swings (glycemic variability) differs meaningfully from person to person and day to day. The study set out to identify which everyday lifestyle factors, including how much people moved, were tied to smoother versus spikier glucose patterns, treating movement as a normal, trackable part of the picture rather than something only relevant to people managing a diagnosed condition.

What it means for your workday

This is part of the reasoning behind the Steps challenge. Step count is one of the easiest things to track consistently, and it's also directly tied to how your body handles glucose day to day. It's the same underlying principle as the Lunch Walk challenge, just measured over a full month instead of a single post-lunch walk: consistent daily movement, not a single big workout, is what shows up as steadier glucose patterns.
