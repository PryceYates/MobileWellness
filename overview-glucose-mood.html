Overview

Glucose, energy, and mood

A randomized crossover feeding study on how the glycemic load of what you eat affects mood and energy in healthy adults, not just people managing diabetes.

What the study looked at

Eighty-two healthy weight and overweight adults, all otherwise healthy, went through a randomized, crossover controlled feeding study comparing a low-glycemic-load diet against a high-glycemic-load diet. Each diet phase ran 28 days. The diets were matched for calories and macronutrients, so the only real difference was how quickly the carbohydrates in each raised blood sugar. Participants filled out standard mood and energy questionnaires at the end of each phase.

What they found

The low-glycemic-load phase produced significantly higher scores for vigor and activity, and significantly lower scores for fatigue, compared to the high-glycemic-load phase. In plain terms: the exact same person, eating the exact same number of calories, reported feeling noticeably more energetic and less fatigued when their diet produced flatter blood sugar swings.

The researchers also pointed to related population-level findings: an analysis of the Women's Health Initiative found that people eating higher-glycemic-index diets had meaningfully higher odds of developing depression over time, and a separate analysis linked inflammatory, high-glycemic dietary patterns to elevated depression risk in the Nurses' Health Study.

What it means for your workday

This is the study behind the Lunch Walk challenge. Lunch is usually the single biggest glucose event of the workday, and what happens to your blood sugar in the hour or two afterward is closely tied to the fatigue and fogginess a lot of people write off as a normal afternoon slump. A short walk right after eating is one of the simplest available tools for flattening that swing, which is exactly why the challenge is timed where it is.
