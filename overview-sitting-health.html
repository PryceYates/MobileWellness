Overview

The cardiovascular case for moving more

A meta-analysis of nine studies and over 448,000 participants on the relationship between daily sitting time and cardiovascular disease and diabetes.

What the study looked at

Researchers pooled data from nine studies covering 448,285 participants, examining the relationship between total daily sitting time and two outcomes: cardiovascular disease and type 2 diabetes. Because the studies were large and varied in design, the analysis controlled for other factors, including how much physical activity people were already getting.

What they found

Higher total daily sitting time was linked to a 29% higher risk of cardiovascular disease and a 13% higher risk of diabetes. The diabetes risk held steady even after accounting for how physically active someone was otherwise, meaning exercise didn't erase the risk that came from the sitting itself. The cardiovascular risk shrank somewhat once physical activity was factored in, but stayed statistically significant either way.

The practical implication the researchers drew is straightforward: total sitting time appears to matter as its own factor, separate from whether someone hits their weekly exercise targets. Being active in the evening doesn't fully offset nine hours of sitting during the day.

What it means for your workday

This is the study behind the cardiovascular stat on Momentum's home page, and it's a big part of the reasoning for why the frameworks here focus on breaking up sitting throughout the day rather than just encouraging exercise before or after work. Small, frequent interruptions, the kind built into Elite Habits or Default Settings, target the specific risk this research identifies, rather than trying to counteract it with a single workout.
