Overview

Why teams outlast leaderboards

A conceptual review of self-determination theory, the leading motivational framework used to explain why some workplace habits stick and others fizzle.

What the paper looked at

This review synthesizes decades of research applying self-determination theory (SDT) to workplace settings. SDT's core idea is that sustained motivation depends on three psychological needs being met: autonomy (choice and ownership), competence (a sense of capability), and relatedness (genuine connection to others).

What they found

Across the research reviewed, autonomous motivation (doing something because it feels self-chosen and meaningful) consistently predicted better performance, satisfaction, and engagement at work. Controlled motivation (doing something because of external pressure or reward) was linked instead to higher burnout and turnover over time.

One detail stands out for team-based habits specifically: relatedness doesn't happen automatically just because people work at the same company. The review notes that employees working in person tend to report a stronger sense of belonging and connection than remote workers doing the same job, which affects how engaged they stay. Relatedness has to be actively built into how work happens, not assumed.

What it means for your workday

This is the research behind The Team Pulse. A leaderboard leans on controlled motivation (external comparison and reward), which this research links to burnout rather than durable engagement. A shared, low-stakes check-in ritual leans on relatedness instead, which the data suggests holds up better over time. It's also why Momentum's challenges are scored as team averages rather than individual rankings: see Lunch Walk or Steps for that in practice.
