Overview

The 10-minute walk that blunts a glucose spike

A randomized crossover trial testing exactly how much walking, and when, is needed to meaningfully lower blood sugar after eating.

What the study looked at

Researchers tested whether a short walk taken immediately after eating could control post-meal blood glucose as effectively as a longer walk taken later. Healthy participants completed the study under three conditions on different days: sitting the whole time, walking for 10 minutes immediately after eating, and walking for 30 minutes starting 30 minutes after eating. Everyone walked at a self-selected, comfortable pace, averaging about 2.4 miles per hour, roughly how people naturally stroll.

What they found

Both walking conditions reduced overall blood glucose exposure compared to sitting. But only the 10-minute walk taken immediately after eating significantly lowered the peak glucose spike itself, 164.3 mg/dL versus 181.9 mg/dL for the no-walk condition, a meaningfully large effect. The 30-minute walk that started later didn't produce a significant reduction in that peak. Participants also rated the short, immediate walk as noticeably easier than the longer one.

What it means for your workday

This is the mechanism behind the Lunch Walk challenge, and it's good news for anyone short on time: you don't need a 30-minute walk to get a meaningful benefit. The timing matters more than the duration. A short walk taken right after lunch, not a long one saved for later in the afternoon, is what the evidence points to as the more effective move.
