Overview

Why "who you are" changes what you repeat

A meta-analysis of nineteen studies and over 13,000 participants on the relationship between identity and habit strength.

What the study looked at

Researchers combined data from nineteen studies (more than 13,000 participants total) looking at physical activity, healthy eating, and drinking behaviors. Each study measured two things: how strongly someone identified with a behavior ("I am someone who exercises") and how automatic or habitual that behavior had become.

What they found

The link between identity and habit was large: a correlation of 0.55, which is a strong effect in behavioral research. People who saw a behavior as part of who they were consistently showed stronger habit strength than people who saw the same behavior as something they were just trying to do. The effect was even larger when identity was measured through direct, explicit self-report ("I think of myself as...") rather than indirect, automatic association tests.

Importantly, the researchers found evidence the relationship isn't one-directional. A strong identity can drive habit formation, but the reverse also showed up in the data: simply repeating a behavior consistently, over time, can reshape how someone sees themselves, even without deliberately working on identity at all.

What it means for your workday

This is the core research behind Say It and Become It. Because the identity-habit link is bidirectional, you don't have to wait until you fully believe an identity statement before it starts working: showing up consistently can build the identity on its own. Pairing this framework with a daily or weekly challenge, like the Two-Minute Trigger, gives both directions of that loop something to work with at once.
