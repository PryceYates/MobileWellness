The Science

The research behind the frameworks

Every framework and challenge on this site is grounded in real, published research, not just intuition. Here are the actual readings, with overviews and links to the original studies.

Behavior science

Why your brain treats sitting as the default

The path of least resistance isn't laziness, it's design. This systematic review lays out how environmental cues and everyday friction shape which behaviors become automatic, and which ones quietly fade.

Habit formation

The science behind small resets

A research agenda from eighteen habit scientists on how habits actually form, including how consistent cues, context, and self-monitoring interact to make a behavior stick.

Identity & habit

Why "who you are" changes what you repeat

A meta-analysis of nineteen studies and over 13,000 participants on the link between identity and habit strength, and why the relationship runs in both directions.

Motivation theory

Why teams outlast leaderboards

A conceptual review of self-determination theory in the workplace, on why relatedness and autonomy tend to outperform reward-based motivation over time.

Workplace wellness

What actually works, and what doesn't

The largest randomized controlled trial of a workplace wellness program to date: 160 worksites, 18 months. A plain look at where these programs help, and where they fall short.

Read an overview Read the study →

Sitting & health

The cardiovascular case for moving more

A meta-analysis of nine studies and over 448,000 participants on the link between daily sitting time and cardiovascular disease and diabetes risk, independent of exercise.

Read an overview Read the study →

Glucose & mood

Glucose, energy, and mood

A randomized crossover feeding study on healthy adults found lower-glycemic-load diets produced significantly more energy and less fatigue than higher-glycemic-load diets, at identical calories.

Read an overview Read the study →

Glucose & movement

Glucose levels and daily movement

A two-week study tracking continuous glucose readings alongside daily step counts in healthy, nondiabetic adults, looking at which everyday habits relate to steadier blood sugar.

Read an overview Read the study →

Glucose regulation

The 10-minute walk that blunts a glucose spike

A randomized crossover trial found a 10-minute walk immediately after eating significantly reduced peak blood glucose, while a longer 30-minute walk taken later did not.
