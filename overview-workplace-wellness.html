Overview

What actually works, and what doesn't

The largest randomized controlled trial of a workplace wellness program to date, published in JAMA: an honest look at what these programs can and can't deliver.

What the study looked at

Researchers from Harvard and the University of Chicago ran an 18-month randomized trial across 160 worksites at a large U.S. retailer, covering more than 32,000 employees. Some worksites got a structured, dietitian-led wellness program with modules on things like exercise and weight management; others didn't. Employees weren't required to participate: this tested the real-world effect of simply offering the program.

What they found

Employees offered the program reported higher rates of regular exercise and actively managing their weight compared to the control group, a genuine, measurable shift in self-reported behavior. But that shift didn't carry through to the outcomes employers usually care about most: no significant change in blood pressure, cholesterol, body mass index, absenteeism, job performance, or healthcare spending over the 18-month window.

The honest takeaway from the researchers wasn't that wellness programs don't work at all: it's that this kind of program, over this timeframe, moved self-reported behavior without moving the clinical or financial outcomes it's often sold on.

What it means for your workday

This study is a big part of why Momentum isn't built like a typical vendor wellness program. It's part of why the frameworks and challenges here emphasize small, specific behaviors, like Elite Habits, rather than a broad, generic initiative that's hard to measure. And it's why the monthly team challenge tracks a simple, concrete behavior instead of promising a health outcome this kind of pilot realistically can't guarantee in a month.
