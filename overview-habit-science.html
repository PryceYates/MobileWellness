Overview

The science behind small resets

Eighteen habit researchers laid out the open questions that matter most for building habit-based interventions that actually work.

What the paper looked at

This isn't a single experiment — it's a research agenda. Eighteen behavioral scientists who study habit formation got together and identified twenty-one open questions that anyone designing a habit-based intervention should be able to answer, covering everything from how habits are measured to how context and environment shape whether they take hold.

What they found

A recurring theme across the paper is that most health research still measures habit through self-report — simply asking people to reflect on their own behavior — which likely misses a lot of what's actually happening automatically, below conscious awareness. The authors also emphasize that habits are highly context-dependent: a behavior tied tightly to one specific situation (same place, same time, same trigger) is far more likely to become automatic than one that has to work across many different settings.

The paper also flags self-monitoring — deliberately tracking a behavior as you do it — as a mechanism worth taking seriously, both for its role in reinforcing the habit loop and for the motivational boost that comes from seeing progress accumulate.

What it means for your workday

This research underpins two Momentum frameworks directly. The Default Setting is built on the context-dependency finding — if your environment's defaults already point toward movement, you don't need as much conscious effort to follow through. And The Momentum Ledger is a direct application of the self-monitoring research — a simple, visible way to track consistency instead of relying on memory alone.
